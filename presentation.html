Q1 · Who is breathing the worst air right now?

Measured OpenAQ readings: India 78 µg/m³ and Pakistan 75 sit ~15× the WHO annual guideline of 5 µg/m³. The United States (9.8) and Israel (12) are the only focus countries near it. Six of eight exceed the guideline.

Q2 · Is this a global problem?

97.8%

of 227 countries with modeled 2023 data exceed the WHO 5 µg/m³ guideline. The most exposed — Qatar, Nigeria, Egypt, Cameroon — run 10–12× the guideline. Clean air is the global exception, not the rule.

Q3 · Is air quality improving?

Direction is encouraging where policy bit hardest: China −47% (2010→2023), Pakistan −32%, India −26%, Israel −30%. But India and Pakistan remain ~43 µg/m³. The US edged up +6% (wildfire smoke), and Egypt stayed flat — progress is uneven.

Q4 · Does dirtier air mean a bigger health toll?

Across 179 countries, PM2.5 vs the share of deaths from outdoor air pollution correlates only weakly (r = 0.27). The reason is instructive: in wealthy nations other causes dominate the death mix, so a share metric understates exposure. Absolute burden tells the real story →

Q6 · The economics: wealth buys clean air

At region/income level, GDP per capita and PM2.5 are strongly negatively correlated (r = -0.702, n=38). Richer regions are cleaner — consistent with the environmental-Kuznets pattern where pollution falls once income funds enforcement and cleaner energy.

Why PM2.5 happens — and why it rose

PM2.5 = particles ≤2.5 µm — small enough to cross from lungs into the bloodstream, which is why it drives heart and lung disease. It comes from combustion and dust:

SourceWhat it is
Fossil-fuel combustionCoal power, industry, road transport — ~41% of all air-pollution deaths globally (IHME 2015)
Household solid fuelsWood/dung/coal for cooking & heating — the indoor share that also leaks outdoors
Open burningCrop-stubble burning, waste fires, and wildfires (the driver behind the US uptick)
Dust & secondary particlesConstruction/road dust, plus particles formed in the air from SO₂, NOₓ and farm ammonia

Why South Asia climbed and stayed high: rapid industrialization, a coal-heavy grid, surging vehicle fleets, and seasonal stubble burning — growth that outpaced emission controls. This is the same force behind the −0.70 wealth↔PM2.5 link: pollution rises in the build-out phase, then falls once income funds cleaner energy and enforcement.

How to lower it — what the data says works

  1. Clean the grid. Coal → renewables/gas. This is what produced China's −47% in 13 years.
  2. Decarbonize transport. Tighter emission & fuel-quality standards; electrify fleets and buses.
  3. Control industry. Scrubbers, filters, and — critically — enforcement of existing limits.
  4. Swap household fuels. LPG/electric cooking cuts the indoor share and outdoor leakage.
  5. End open burning. Stubble-management incentives; ammonia controls in agriculture.
  6. Measure & coordinate. Dense monitoring (e.g. OpenAQ) and regional airshed cooperation — pollution crosses borders.

The evidence in this dataset is hopeful: the trend is bendable, not fixed. China's drop shows concentrated energy + industrial policy can halve concentrations within a decade.

But the r = −0.70 wealth link is the catch — the dirtiest countries are the least able to finance the switch, so cleaning South Asia's ~43 µg/m³ air is as much a financing question as a technical one.

Scenario simulator — what would investment buy?

An interactive what-if tool (scenario_dashboard.html): pick a country's real baseline, then drag four levers — solar, EVs, clean household fuels, stop open burning — to project PM2.5 over 10 years with cost and lives-saved estimates.

Worked example — India, all four levers fully deployed:

  1. PM2.5 42.5 → 22.4 µg/m³ (−47%) over 10 years
  2. Illustrative cost ~$180 B across the program
  3. ~3.2 million cumulative deaths averted

The honest ceiling: even maxing these four levers leaves India at ~22 µg/m³ — still 4× the WHO guideline, because industry and background dust/transboundary PM2.5 aren't touched by them. Reaching WHO 5 needs industrial controls and regional cooperation on top.

Baselines are real (WHO/IHME). Sector split and costs are transparent, editable assumptions — the data has no source-apportionment or cost figures, so this is a reasoning tool, not a forecast.

So what — three takeaways

  1. Exposure is near-universal: 98% of countries breach the WHO guideline; South Asia & the Gulf are 10× over.
  2. The trend is bendable: China's −47% proves concentrated policy works — but India/Pakistan still carry ~1M deaths/yr each.
  3. It tracks development: wealth and clean air move together (r=−0.70), so the burden falls hardest on lower-income regions least able to absorb it.
